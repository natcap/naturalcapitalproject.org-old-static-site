Ecosystem Services

  • Cultural Heritage
    Chiloé's traditional farming practices are world-renowned, and famous for growing over 200 varieties of potato. InVEST's Sediment Retention Model can help identify best management practices for traditional farms.

  • Habitat Protection
    Shorebirds about on Chiloe. They are a popular tourist attraction, and many birds rely on Chiloé's wetlands for feeding and breeding grounds. InVEST's Habitat Risk Assessment model is helping to identify which wetlands may be threatened by urban sprawl.

  • Recreation & Tourism
    Tourism is one of the primary economic sectors in Chiloé, people come from around the world to see their penguins. CECPAN is using InVEST's Recreation and Scenic Quality Models to demonstrate the value of tourism now and in the future.

Protecting the Chiloé Archipelago in Chile

Supporting conservation with scientific guidance

Since 2013, NatCap has provided scientific guidance and support to the Center for Studies and Conservation of Natural Heritage, a nongovernmental organization known by its acronym in Spanish, CECPAN. This collaboration, funded by the David and Lucile Packard Foundation, backs initiatives to conserve various elements of cultural and natural heritage in the archipelago of Chiloé in Southern Chile. NatCap continues to guide CECPAN staff in how to use our approach and tools, apply InVEST models in novel ways to address their needs, and provide feedback on several conservation initiatives.



Our Work Together in Chiloe


Designating the Lacuy Peninsula as a Zone of Tourist Interest

In one of its current initiatives, CECPAN collaborates with the National Tourism Service (SERNATUR), the Municipality of Ancud, the Association of Tourism of Ancud, and the broader community to designate the Lacuy Peninsula as a Zone of Tourist Interest. Colonies of Humboldt and Magellanic penguins, among other marine fauna, draw more than 19,000 visitors annually to this region of significant natural and cultural heritage. Consequently, tourism and recreation constitute a vital piece of the local economy. Given that a large portion of the peninsula is now under consideration for a major wind farm project, CECPAN is working to ensure that impacts to recreation, shorebirds, and local communities are taken into account during decision making processes. CECPAN applied the InVEST Recreation and Scenic Quality models to show decision makers how important the region is for recreation and tourism, and how important the peninsula could prove in the future. The goal of this collaboration is to officially recognize the Lacuy Peninsula's role in recreation and tourism. Its designation as a Zone of Tourist Interest will help conserve the coastal ecosystems that support local communities.


Preserving Agricultural Herritage

Elsewhere on the archipelago, CECPAN is demonstrating the value of rural land in an effort to regulate urban sprawl and preserve small scale farming. Chiloé's ancestral farming practices, including the cultivation of some 200 varieties of native potatoes, garnered recognition as a Global Important Agricultural Heritage System by FAO and UNESCO. CECPAN is working with the Agricultural Development Institute (INDAP) and Local Development Programs (PRODESAL), the Municipality of Castro, the National Forest Corporation (CONAF), the Center of Education and Technology (CET), and small scale farmers to promote farming practices that reduce soil erosion, including hedges of native forest. CECPAN applied the Sediment Retention model to show the benefits of a best management practices scenario. This collaboration aims to secure and leverage government funds to promote these practices that conserve soil, and, in turn, agricultural heritage.


Protecting Wetlands for Shorebirds

In these same sites, CECPAN is using the Habitat Risk Assessment model to project the effects of a business as usual scenario of urban sprawl into rural wetlands near Castro, the capital of the Chiloé province. These coastal wetlands serve as critical habitat for migratory shorebirds and underscore a need for careful planning that regulates residential development in rural areas adjacent to city perimeters. In collaboration with the Municipality of Castro and local residents, CECPAN is working to ensure that considerations of critical habitat enter into zoning measures.

Protecting Endangered Species

One of the next projects CECPAN plans to tackle concerns Darwin's Fox (Zorro de Darwin), a critically endangered fox that is only found on Chiloe Island and in a nearby area on the mainland. Only about 250 Darwin's Foxes are left in the world, the vast majority of which live on Chiloe Island. Mainland foxes are threatened by the invasive American mink that competes with the fox for food; locals are concerned that the mink will invade Chiloe Island as well. CECPAN is planning to use the InVEST Habitat Risk Assessment model to pinpoint areas on Chiloe Island that would be prime habitat for the mink, identifying areas at most risk for invasion. This invasion assessment can then be used to concentrate monitoring and control efforts in locations most likely to contain minks.
