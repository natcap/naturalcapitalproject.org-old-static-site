A Network for the Conservation of River Ecosystems of Patagonia


Patagonia is covered in hundreds of beautiful blue lakes and flowing rivers, and is home to some of the largest ice fields in the world. From the surface, it may appear that water-related issues are not a problem for a region blessed with freshwater resources, but a closer look reveals a much more complex picture. Population growth, urban development and agriculture are transforming river basins. The last few free-flowing rivers in the region are about to be dammed, and mining activities continue to grow. Patagonia was once known for is flyfishing, but now many rivers and lakes are no longer able to sustain the famed recreational fisheries of the past. Freshwater is becoming a limited resource in cities, and the situation is predicted to grow worse.

The livelihood and lifestyle of the people of Patagonia are closely tied to the natural capital in the region, particularly to their water resources. Our work in Patagonia attempts to make the connection between healthy rivers and human well-being self-evident.

This project is designed to advance freshwater conservation of 3 major rivers in Patagonia: the Chimehuin River in northwestern Patagonia, the Chubut River in central Patagonia, and the Grande River in southern Patagonia. We are in the process of creating a technical hub at the Patagonian National Center (CENPAT-CONICET) that will conduct ecosystem level research aimed at freshwater conservation and promote the conservation of Patagonian rivers, and forming a network of institutions to promote ecosuystem level conservation and management of rivers in Patagonia. Over the next 5 years, we will be assessing the real coasts and benefits associated with different water practices, identifying sustainable water resources practices, and sharing our knowledge of how to engage in sustainable water-use practices with the people of Patagonia.


A Changing Landscape


Here you can see a pair of photographs of the Chimehuin River valley, with a view of the Lanin volcano in the background. The top photograph was taken in 1970, and the bottom photograph was taken from the same spot in 2012. One of the most striking differences between the photographs is a substantial change in vegetation, mostly associated with exotic willows invading riparian habitat and some pine plantations. It's likely that these trees are affecting the hydrology, sediment retention, nutrient cycling, and even the morphology of the river itself. This project aims to study those impacts and see how the system has changed, and how it might continue to change in the future.
